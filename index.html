Lockheed Martin - 1944

The F-80 Shooting Star was the first operational jet fighter used by the United States Air Force (USAF). Developed by Lockheed during World War II, it was designed to compete with advanced German jet aircraft like the Messerschmitt Me 262. The prototype, designated XP-80, flew for the first time in January 1944.
The F-80 featured a single-engine, straight-wing design and was powered by a centrifugal-flow turbojet. It had a maximum speed of approximately 600 mph (965 km/h) and a range of about 1,000 miles (1,609 km). Armed with six .50-caliber machine guns, it could also carry bombs and rockets, making it versatile for both air-to-air combat and ground-attack missions.

Republic - 1947

The F-84 Thunderjet was an early jet-powered fighter-bomber developed by Republic Aviation for the United States Air Force (USAF). Designed in the mid-1940s, it was introduced as a straight-wing aircraft and later evolved into more advanced swept-wing variants. The first prototype flew in 1946, and it entered service in 1947.
The F-84 was powered by a turbojet engine, achieving a maximum speed of around 620 mph (1,000 km/h) with a range of approximately 1,000 miles (1,600 km). Its robust airframe allowed it to carry a significant payload of bombs, rockets, and even tactical nuclear weapons, making it a versatile fighter-bomber.

North American - 1947

The F-86 Sabre was a transonic jet fighter developed by North American Aviation in the early 1940s. It became one of the most iconic and successful fighters of the Cold War era, serving as the primary U.S. fighter during the Korean War. The F-86 was designed to counter the Soviet MiG-15, which had superior performance in early jet combat.
The F-86 featured a swept-wing design, which was a major innovation at the time, providing enhanced maneuverability at high speeds. Powered by a turbojet engine, the F-86 had a top speed of around 685 mph (1,100 km/h) and a combat radius of 400 miles (640 km). It was armed with six .50-caliber machine guns and could carry rockets or bombs for ground-attack missions.

North American - 1953

The F-100 Super Sabre was the first aircraft in the United States Air Force (USAF) to exceed the speed of sound in level flight and became one of the cornerstone fighters of the Cold War era. Developed by North American Aviation, it was a natural evolution of the F-86 Sabre, featuring a more powerful engine, a supersonic design, and advanced avionics.
Introduced in 1954, the F-100 had a swept-wing design and was powered by a turbojet engine, which gave it a top speed of around 1,200 mph (1,930 km/h), making it one of the fastest aircraft of its time. It had a combat radius of approximately 500 miles (800 km), and could carry a wide range of munitions, including bombs, rockets, and nuclear weapons.

McDonnell Douglas - 1958

The F-4 Phantom II is one of the most versatile and iconic jet fighters in aviation history. Developed by McDonnell Aircraft for the United States Navy, it was first introduced in 1960 and quickly became one of the most successful and widely used aircraft during the Cold War and beyond.
The F-4 was a tandem two-seat, twin-engine aircraft with a distinctive fuselage design. It was powered by two turbojet engines, which allowed it to reach speeds over Mach 2 (1,400 mph or 2,250 km/h). Its range was approximately 1,500 miles (2,400 km). The aircraft was equipped with advanced avionics, radar systems, and could carry a wide array of weapons, including air-to-air missiles (like the AIM-7 Sparrow and AIM-9 Sidewinder), bombs, and rockets.

Northrop - 1958

The F-5 Freedom Fighter is the original version of the Northrop F-5 series, developed in the late 1950s as a low-cost, light fighter for air forces with limited budgets. Designed for air-to-air combat and ground attack, the F-5 was intended to be a simple, easy-to-maintain aircraft that could still deliver solid performance. It became one of the most successful and widely used jet fighters in the world due to its affordability and reliability.
Introduced in 1962, the F-5A Freedom Fighter was powered by two J85-GE-13 turbojet engines, allowing it to reach speeds of 1,050 mph (1,690 km/h). Its combat radius was around 650 miles (1,050 km), and it could carry a variety of weapons, including rockets, bombs, and air-to-air missiles.

Grumman - 1970

The F-14 Tomcat is a legendary carrier-based fighter aircraft developed by Grumman for the United States Navy. Introduced in the early 1970s, the F-14 became one of the most iconic and versatile jet fighters of the Cold War era, known for its distinctive variable-sweep wing design, impressive performance, and long service life.
Powered by two Pratt & Whitney TF30 turbofan engines (later upgraded to GE F110 engines in later models), the F-14 had a maximum speed of around 1,550 mph (2,500 km/h). The F-14 was a long-range interceptor and could carry a variety of air-to-air and air-to-ground weapons. The F-14 was also capable of carrying bombs, rockets, and precision-guided munitions, making it a highly versatile platform for both long-range air-to-air and strike missions.

McDonnell Douglas - 1972

The F-15 Eagle is a highly advanced, all-weather, tactical fighter aircraft developed by McDonnell Douglas. First introduced in the mid-1970s, the F-15 was designed to excel in air superiority missions, ensuring dominance in the skies. Its primary role is to engage and destroy enemy aircraft, but it is also capable of performing ground-attack missions.
The F-15 is powered by two Pratt & Whitney F100-PW-100 engines, allowing it to achieve speeds of over Mach 2.5 (1,650 mph or 2,660 km/h). It has a combat radius of around 2,400 miles (3,860 km) with external fuel tanks, giving it long-range capabilities. The aircraft is armed with a variety of air-to-air missiles, including AIM-7 Sparrow, AIM-120 AMRAAM, and AIM-9 Sidewinder, as well as a M61 Vulcan cannon.

General Dynamics - 1974

The F-16 Fighting Falcon is a highly versatile, multirole fighter aircraft developed by General Dynamics (now part of Lockheed Martin) for the United States Air Force. First introduced in 1978, the F-16 was designed to be a lightweight, affordable fighter capable of performing a wide range of missions, including air superiority, ground attack, and close air support.
The F-16 is powered by a Pratt & Whitney F100-PW-200 turbofan engine, enabling it to reach speeds of up to Mach 2 (1,320 mph or 2,120 km/h). It has a combat radius of approximately 300-400 miles (480-640 km), with external fuel tanks extending its range. The aircraft is highly maneuverable due to its fly-by-wire flight control system, which provides exceptional agility and stability, even at high speeds and in challenging conditions.

McDonnell Douglas - 1978

The F/A-18 Hornet is a versatile, carrier-based multirole fighter aircraft developed by McDonnell Douglas (now part of Boeing) for the United States Navy. First introduced in 1983, the F/A-18 was designed to perform both air-to-air and air-to-ground combat roles, making it highly adaptable for a wide range of missions.
The F/A-18 features a supersonic speed of Mach 1.8 (1,190 mph or 1,915 km/h) and a combat radius of approximately 400-500 miles (640-800 km), depending on the mission and configuration. It is powered by a General Electric F404 turbofan engine, giving it exceptional maneuverability and agility, especially in carrier operations, where space and precision are critical.

Lockheed Martin - 1978

The F-22 Raptor is a fifth-generation, stealth, multirole fighter aircraft developed by Lockheed Martin for the United States Air Force. First introduced in 2005, the F-22 is renowned for its combination of stealth, supercruise, advanced avionics, and exceptional maneuverability.
The F-22 is powered by two Pratt & Whitney F119-PW-100 turbofan engines, enabling it to achieve speeds of Mach 2.25 (1,500 mph or 2,414 km/h). It has a combat radius of around 460 miles (740 km), which can be extended with external fuel tanks. The F-22 is equipped with advanced avionics, including an active electronically scanned array (AESA) radar, and a range of air-to-air and air-to-ground weapons

Lockheed Martin - 2006

The F-35 Lightning II is a family of fifth-generation, multirole, stealth fighter aircraft developed by Lockheed Martin for the United States and allied nations. Designed to perform a wide range of missions, including air superiority, strike, intelligence gathering, and electronic warfare, the F-35 is one of the most advanced and versatile fighter aircraft in the world.
The F-35 is powered by a Pratt & Whitney F135 turbofan engine, providing the aircraft with speeds exceeding Mach 1.6 (around 1,200 mph or 1,930 km/h) and a combat radius of approximately 600-700 miles (965-1,130 km). The F-35 is also equipped with advanced weapons systems, capable of carrying a variety of air-to-air and air-to-ground munitions. The aircraft also has an internal weapons bay to preserve its stealth characteristics.
